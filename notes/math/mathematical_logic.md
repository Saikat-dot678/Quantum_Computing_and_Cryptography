Propositional Logic 🏛️1.1 What Is a Proposition? 📝A proposition is a statement that is either true or false, but not both.Examples:"$2 + 3 = 5$" is a proposition. It is true."7 is even" is a proposition. It is false."Close the door" is not a proposition. It is a command."Is 5 prime?" is not a proposition. It is a question."$x > 3$" is not a proposition until we know what $x$ is.A proposition must have a definite truth value.1.2 Propositional Variables 🔠Instead of writing long statements repeatedly, we use symbols.Let:$$p = \text{“It is raining.”}$$$$q = \text{“The ground is wet.”}$$Then we can write logical expressions using $p$ and $q$.1.3 Logical Connectives 🔗Logical connectives combine propositions. The main connectives are:SymbolNameMeaning$\neg p$NOT $p$$p$ is false$p \land q$$p$ AND $q$both are true$p \lor q$$p$ OR $q$at least one is true$p \to q$if $p$, then $q$$p$ implies $q$$p \leftrightarrow q$$p$ iff $q$both have same truth value1.4 Negation 🚫The negation of $p$, written $\neg p$, means “not $p$.”If $p = \text{“5 is prime”}$, then $\neg p = \text{“5 is not prime”}$. Since $p$ is true, $\neg p$ is false.Truth table:p¬pTFFT1.5 Conjunction: AND 🤝$p \land q$ means both $p$ and $q$ are true.Example:$p = \text{“2 is even”}$$q = \text{“3 is prime”}$Then $p \land q = \text{“2 is even and 3 is prime”}$This is true because both parts are true.Truth table:pqp∧qTTTTFFFTFFFFAND is strict. One false part makes the whole statement false.1.6 Disjunction: OR 🔀$p \lor q$ means at least one of $p$ or $q$ is true. In mathematics, OR is usually inclusive. So $p \lor q$ is true when $p$ is true, $q$ is true, or both are true.Truth table:pqp∨qTTTTFTFTTFFFExample:“7 is prime or 7 is even.” This is true because 7 is prime, even though it is not even.1.7 Implication ➡️The implication $p \to q$ means: If $p$ is true, then $q$ must be true.Here:$p$ is the hypothesis or antecedent.$q$ is the conclusion or consequent.Example:$p = \text{“n is divisible by 4”}$$q = \text{“n is even”}$$p \to q$ means: If $n$ is divisible by 4, then $n$ is even. This implication is true.1.8 Truth Table for Implication 📊pqp→qTTTTFFFTTFFTThe only time $p \to q$ is false is when $p$ is true and $q$ is false.Why? Because an implication promises: Whenever $p$ happens, $q$ happens. If $p$ does not happen, the promise is not broken.Example:If I score 100, I will give you ₹500. If I do not score 100, then I did not break the promise, no matter whether I give ₹500 or not. This idea is extremely important in mathematics and cryptography.1.9 Converse, Inverse, and Contrapositive 🔄For an implication $p \to q$, we define:NameFormOriginal$p \to q$Converse$q \to p$Inverse$\neg p \to \neg q$Contrapositive$\neg q \to \neg p$The original and contrapositive are logically equivalent:$$p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$$But the converse and inverse are not generally equivalent to the original.Example:Original: If $n$ is divisible by 4, then $n$ is even. (True)Converse: If $n$ is even, then $n$ is divisible by 4. (False, e.g., 6 is even but not divisible by 4)Contrapositive: If $n$ is not even, then $n$ is not divisible by 4. (True)Contrapositive proofs are very common in mathematics.1.10 Biconditional: If and Only If ↔️$p \leftrightarrow q$ means $p \to q$ and $q \to p$. So $p \leftrightarrow q$ means $p$ and $q$ are either both true or both false.Example:$$n \text{ is even} \leftrightarrow n^2 \text{ is even}$$This means:If $n$ is even, then $n^2$ is even.If $n^2$ is even, then $n$ is even.Both are true. In proofs, “if and only if” requires proving both directions.1.11 Logical Equivalence ⚖️Two propositions are logically equivalent if they always have the same truth value. We write $p \equiv q$ or $p \Leftrightarrow q$.Example:$$p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$$This equivalence is very important. Why? Because “if $p$, then $q$” is false only when $p$ is true and $q$ is false. So it is true when either $p$ is false or $q$ is true.1.12 De Morgan’s Laws 📉De Morgan’s laws tell us how to negate AND and OR.$$\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$$$$\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$$Meaning:Not both $p$ and $q$ are true means at least one of them is false.Neither $p$ nor $q$ is true means both are false.Example:Original: It is not true that $x > 0$ and $y > 0$.Equivalent: $x \le 0$ or $y \le 0$.1.13 Common Logical Equivalences 📚LawFormulaDouble negation$\neg(\neg p) \equiv p$Commutative AND$p \land q \equiv q \land p$Commutative OR$p \lor q \equiv q \lor p$Associative AND$(p \land q) \land r \equiv p \land (q \land r)$Associative OR$(p \lor q) \lor r \equiv p \lor (q \lor r)$Distributive$p \land(q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q)\lor(p \land r)$Distributive$p \lor(q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q)\land(p \lor r)$Implication$p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$Contrapositive$p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$1.14 Tautology and Contradiction 🎯A tautology is always true.Example: $p \lor \neg p$ (Law of excluded middle).A contradiction is always false.Example: $p \land \neg p$.A statement that is sometimes true and sometimes false is called a contingency.1.15 Why Propositional Logic Matters in Computing 💻In computer science:Boolean circuits use AND, OR, NOT.Program conditions use logic.Digital gates use propositional logic.SAT solvers solve logical formulas.Formal verification checks whether systems satisfy logical properties.In cryptography:Security games are defined using logical statements.Proofs often show that if an adversary breaks one system, then another assumption is false.Reductions use implications.In quantum computing:Classical control logic is used in quantum circuits.Measurement outcomes are classical bits.Many algorithms require logical reasoning about states and operations.2. Predicate Logic 🔍Propositional logic treats statements as whole units. Predicate logic goes deeper. It allows us to talk about objects and properties.2.1 Predicates 🏷️A predicate is a statement containing variables.Example:$$P(x): x \text{ is prime}$$This is not true or false until $x$ is specified.If $x = 7$, then $P(7)$ means “7 is prime,” which is true.If $x = 8$, then $P(8)$ means “8 is prime,” which is false.2.2 Domain of Discourse 🌍The domain is the set of objects we are talking about.Example:$$P(x): x > 0$$If the domain is natural numbers $\mathbb{N} = \{1,2,3,\dots\}$, then $P(x)$ is true for every $x$.If the domain is integers $\mathbb{Z} = \{\dots,-2,-1,0,1,2,\dots\}$, then $P(x)$ is not always true.So the truth of a predicate depends on the domain. This is extremely important.2.3 Universal Quantifier ∀The universal quantifier means “for all.” Symbol: $\forall$Example:$$\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, x^2 \ge 0$$Meaning: For every real number $x$, $x^2$ is nonnegative. This statement is true.2.4 Existential Quantifier ∃The existential quantifier means “there exists.” Symbol: $\exists$Example:$$\exists x \in \mathbb{Z} \text{ such that } x^2 = 9$$Meaning: There exists an integer $x$ whose square is 9. This is true because $x = 3$ and $x = -3$ both work.2.5 Unique Existence ❗️Sometimes we need to say exactly one object exists. Symbol: $\exists!$Example:$$\exists! x \in \mathbb{R} \text{ such that } x + 3 = 7$$Meaning: There exists exactly one real number $x$ such that $x + 3 = 7$. This is true because only $x = 4$ works.2.6 Negating Quantifiers 🛑This is one of the most important parts of logic.The negation of “for all” is “there exists a counterexample.”$$\neg(\forall x, P(x)) \equiv \exists x \text{ such that } \neg P(x)$$The negation of “there exists” is “for all, not.”$$\neg(\exists x, P(x)) \equiv \forall x, \neg P(x)$$Example:Original: $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, x^2 > 0$ (Meaning: Every real number has positive square. False because $0^2 = 0$).Negation: $\exists x \in \mathbb{R} \text{ such that } x^2 \le 0$ (Indeed, $x = 0$ is a counterexample).2.7 Negating Inequalities ⚖️You must be careful with inequalities.StatementNegation$x > a$$x \le a$$x \ge a$$x < a$$x < a$$x \ge a$$x \le a$$x > a$$x = a$$x \ne a$$x \ne a$$x = a$Example:Negation of $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, x > 5$ is $\exists x \in \mathbb{R} \text{ such that } x \le 5$.2.8 Nested Quantifiers 🪆Many mathematical statements contain more than one quantifier.Example:$$\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, \exists y \in \mathbb{R} \text{ such that } y > x$$Meaning: For every real number $x$, there exists a real number $y$ bigger than $x$. This is true. Given $x$, choose $y = x + 1$.Now compare:$$\exists y \in \mathbb{R}, \forall x \in \mathbb{R}, y > x$$Meaning: There exists one real number $y$ that is bigger than every real number $x$. This is false. The order of quantifiers matters.2.9 Quantifier Order Matters ⚠️Compare these two statements:Statement A: $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, \exists y \in \mathbb{R}: y = x^2$Meaning: For every $x$, there is some $y$ equal to $x^2$. (True)Statement B: $\exists y \in \mathbb{R}, \forall x \in \mathbb{R}: y = x^2$Meaning: There is one fixed $y$ equal to $x^2$ for every $x$. (False)In cryptography, quantifier order is critical. For example: $\forall A, \exists \epsilon$ does not mean the same as $\exists \epsilon, \forall A$. Security definitions depend heavily on this order.2.10 Translating English into Predicate Logic 🗣️Example: Every prime number greater than 2 is odd.Let $P(n): n \text{ is prime}$ and $O(n): n \text{ is odd}$. Domain: integers.Formal statement:$$\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}, ((P(n) \land n > 2) \to O(n))$$Notice the implication. We do not say $P(n) \land n > 2 \land O(n)$ because that would claim every integer is prime, greater than 2, and odd.2.11 Common Predicate Logic Patterns 🧩Pattern 1: “All A are B”English: All students in the class passed.Formal: $\forall x, Student(x) \to Passed(x)$Pattern 2: “Some A are B”English: Some students passed.Formal: $\exists x, Student(x) \land Passed(x)$Important distinction:Universal statements usually use implication. Existential statements usually use AND.Why? “All A are B” means if something is A, then it is B. “Some A are B” means there exists something that is both A and B.2.12 Negating Complex Predicate Statements 🧩Example: $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, \exists y \in \mathbb{R}: y > x$Negation: $\exists x \in \mathbb{R} \text{ such that } \forall y \in \mathbb{R}, y \le x$Process:Change $\forall$ to $\exists$.Change $\exists$ to $\forall$.Negate the final predicate (e.g., $y > x$ becomes $y \le x$).2.13 Why Predicate Logic Matters for Your Goal 🎯In quantum computing:Quantum states are defined using quantified statements.Operators are defined over vector spaces.Theorems often say “for every vector” or “there exists a basis.”In cryptography:Security definitions are full of quantifiers.Examples: For every adversary, there exists a negligible function, such that for all sufficiently large security parameters, the advantage is small.If you misunderstand quantifier order, you misunderstand the theorem.3. Set Theory 📦Set theory is the language of modern mathematics. Almost every mathematical object can be described using sets.3.1 What Is a Set? 📌A set is a collection of distinct objects.Examples:$$A = \{1,2,3\}$$$$B = \{\text{red}, \text{blue}, \text{green}\}$$The objects inside a set are called elements.If $x$ is an element of $A$, we write: $x \in A$If $x$ is not an element of $A$, we write: $x \notin A$Example:$2 \in \{1,2,3\}$$5 \notin \{1,2,3\}$3.2 Important Number Sets 🔢SymbolMeaning$\mathbb{N}$natural numbers$\mathbb{Z}$integers$\mathbb{Q}$rational numbers$\mathbb{R}$real numbers$\mathbb{C}$complex numbersCommon convention: $\mathbb{N} = \{1,2,3,\dots\}$. Some books include 0 in $\mathbb{N}$. Always check the convention.3.3 Describing Sets ✍️There are two common ways.Roster Form: List the elements. $A = \{1,2,3,4\}$Set-Builder Form: Describe a property. $A = \{x \in \mathbb{Z}: 1 \le x \le 4\}$This means: The set of all integers $x$ such that $1 \le x \le 4$. So $A = \{1,2,3,4\}$.3.4 Empty Set 🕳️The empty set has no elements. Symbols: $\emptyset$ or $\{\}$.Example:$\{x \in \mathbb{Z}: x^2 = -1\} = \emptyset$ because no integer square is -1.Important: $\emptyset \ne \{\emptyset\}$. The set $\emptyset$ has no elements. The set $\{\emptyset\}$ has one element, namely the empty set.3.5 Subsets ⊂A set $A$ is a subset of $B$ if every element of $A$ is also an element of $B$. We write: $A \subseteq B$.Formal definition:$$A \subseteq B \iff \forall x, x \in A \to x \in B$$Example: $\{1,2\} \subseteq \{1,2,3\}$ because every element of $\{1,2\}$ is also in $\{1,2,3\}$.3.6 Proper Subsets ⊆A set $A$ is a proper subset of $B$ if: $A \subseteq B$, and $A \ne B$. Written: $A \subset B$.Example: $\{1,2\} \subset \{1,2,3\}$.3.7 Set Equality =Two sets are equal if they have exactly the same elements.$$A = B \iff \forall x, x \in A \leftrightarrow x \in B$$To prove $A = B$, usually prove: $A \subseteq B$, and $B \subseteq A$. This is called double inclusion.3.8 Union ∪The union of $A$ and $B$ is the set of elements that are in $A$, or in $B$, or in both.$$A \cup B = \{x: x \in A \lor x \in B\}$$Example: $\{1,2,3\} \cup \{3,4,5\} = \{1,2,3,4,5\}$.3.9 Intersection ∩The intersection of $A$ and $B$ is the set of elements common to both.$$A \cap B = \{x: x \in A \land x \in B\}$$Example: $\{1,2,3\} \cap \{3,4,5\} = \{3\}$.3.10 Difference ➖The difference $A - B$, also written $A \setminus B$, is the set of elements in $A$ but not in $B$.$$A \setminus B = \{x: x \in A \land x \notin B\}$$Example: $\{1,2,3\} \setminus \{3,4,5\} = \{1,2\}$.3.11 Complement 🌓If there is a universal set $U$, then the complement of $A$ is:$$A^c = \{x \in U: x \notin A\}$$Example: Let $U = \{1,2,3,4,5\}$ and $A = \{1,2\}$. Then $A^c = \{3,4,5\}$.3.12 De Morgan’s Laws for Sets ⚖️$$(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c$$$$(A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c$$These are set versions of logical De Morgan laws.Why? $x \in (A \cup B)^c$ means $x \notin A \cup B$, meaning $x \notin A \text{ and } x \notin B$, which means $x \in A^c \cap B^c$.3.13 Cartesian Product ✖️The Cartesian product of $A$ and $B$ is the set of ordered pairs:$$A \times B = \{(a,b): a \in A, b \in B\}$$Example: Let $A = \{1,2\}$ and $B = \{x,y\}$.Then $A \times B = \{(1,x),(1,y),(2,x),(2,y)\}$.Ordered pairs care about order: $(a,b) \ne (b,a)$ unless $a=b$.Cartesian products are important for relations, functions, probability spaces, quantum tensor products, and cryptographic key-message pairs.3.14 Power Set 🔋The power set of $A$, written $\mathcal{P}(A)$, is the set of all subsets of $A$.Example: $A = \{1,2\}$.Then $\mathcal{P}(A) = \{\emptyset,\{1\},\{2\},\{1,2\}\}$.If $A$ has $n$ elements, then $|\mathcal{P}(A)| = 2^n$. This is because each element has two choices: included or not included.Power sets appear in combinatorics, probability, Boolean logic, computer science, and quantum measurement outcome sets.3.15 Cardinality 📏The cardinality of a finite set is the number of elements in it. Written: $|A|$.Example: $|\{a,b,c\}| = 3$.For finite sets:$$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$$This is the inclusion-exclusion principle for two sets.3.16 Indexed Families of Sets 📚Sometimes we have many sets: $A_1, A_2, A_3, \dots, A_n$.Their union: $\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i$ means $A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n$Their intersection: $\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} A_i$ means $A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n$This notation is common in probability, analysis, cryptography, and quantum theory.3.17 Partitions 🧩A partition of a set $A$ is a collection of nonempty subsets whose union is $A$, and which do not overlap.Example: $A = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$.A partition is $\{\{1,3,5\}, \{2,4,6\}\}$ because every element of $A$ is included, the two subsets do not overlap, and neither subset is empty. Partitions are closely related to equivalence relations.3.18 Why Set Theory Matters for Your Goal 🚀In quantum computing:A vector space is a set of vectors with operations.A basis is a special set of vectors.Measurement outcomes form sets.Hilbert spaces are sets with algebraic and geometric structure.In cryptography:Key spaces, message spaces, and ciphertext spaces are sets.Security games sample elements from sets.Functions map messages and keys to ciphertexts.Example: Encryption is a function $Enc: \mathcal{K} \times \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{C}$. Without set theory, this notation is impossible to understand properly.4. Relations 🕸️Relations describe how elements are connected.4.1 What Is a Relation? 🔗A binary relation $R$ from set $A$ to set $B$ is a subset of $A \times B$. So $R \subseteq A \times B$.If $(a,b) \in R$, we write $aRb$ and say $a$ is related to $b$.Example: Let $A = \{1,2,3\}$. Define relation $R$ by $aRb \iff a < b$.Then $R = \{(1,2),(1,3),(2,3)\}$.4.2 Relations on a Set 🔄A relation on $A$ is a subset of $A \times A$.Example: Let $A = \{1,2,3\}$. Relation $R = \{(1,1),(2,2),(3,3)\}$. This is the equality relation.4.3 Reflexive Relations 🪞A relation $R$ on $A$ is reflexive if every element is related to itself.Formal definition: $\forall a \in A, aRa$.Example: $\le$ on real numbers is reflexive because $a \le a$. $<$ is not reflexive because $a < a$ is false.4.4 Symmetric Relations 🤝A relation $R$ on $A$ is symmetric if $aRb \to bRa$ for all $a,b \in A$.Example: “has the same birthday as” is symmetric. But $\le$ is not symmetric (if $2 \le 3$, it does not follow that $3 \le 2$).4.5 Antisymmetric Relations 🛡️A relation $R$ on $A$ is antisymmetric if $(aRb \land bRa) \to a=b$.Example: $\le$ is antisymmetric (if $a \le b$ and $b \le a$, then $a=b$).Important: Antisymmetric does not mean “not symmetric.” A relation can be neither symmetric nor antisymmetric.4.6 Transitive Relations 🛤️A relation $R$ is transitive if $(aRb \land bRc) \to aRc$.Example: $\le$ is transitive (if $a \le b$ and $b \le c$, then $a \le c$).4.7 Equivalence Relations ⚖️A relation $R$ on $A$ is an equivalence relation if it is:reflexivesymmetrictransitiveEquivalence relations formalize the idea of “same in some sense.”Example: Define relation on integers: $a \sim b \iff a-b \text{ is divisible by } 3$.Reflexive: $a-a = 0$ (divisible by 3).Symmetric: If $a-b$ is divisible by 3, then $b-a = -(a-b)$ is also divisible by 3.Transitive: If $a-b$ and $b-c$ are divisible by 3, then $a-c = (a-b) + (b-c)$ is divisible by 3.This relation is called congruence modulo 3. Written: $a \equiv b \pmod 3$. Modular arithmetic is central in cryptography.4.8 Equivalence Classes 🗂️Given an equivalence relation, the equivalence class of $a$ is:$$[a] = \{x \in A: x \sim a\}$$For congruence modulo 3, the equivalence classes are:$[0] = \{\dots,-6,-3,0,3,6,\dots\}$$[1] = \{\dots,-5,-2,1,4,7,\dots\}$$[2] = \{\dots,-4,-1,2,5,8,\dots\}$These are the possible remainders modulo 3. Equivalence classes partition the set.4.9 Partial Orders 📊A relation $R$ is a partial order if it is:reflexiveantisymmetrictransitiveExamples: $\le$ on numbers, and $\subseteq$ on sets.4.10 Total Orders 📏A partial order is a total order if every two elements are comparable.For numbers, $a \le b$ or $b \le a$, so $\le$ is a total order.But $\subseteq$ on sets is not always total (e.g., $A = \{1\}$, $B = \{2\}$, neither is a subset of the other).4.11 Relations in Cryptography and Quantum Computing 🔐In cryptography:Modular congruence is an equivalence relation.Security reductions relate problems.Partial orders appear in complexity classes and lattice-based cryptography.Relations define computational problems (e.g., NP verification).In quantum computing:Orthogonality is a relation between vectors.Equality of states up to global phase is an equivalence relation.Measurement outcomes can be related to subspaces.5. Functions ⚙️Functions are among the most important objects in all of mathematics.5.1 What Is a Function? 🎯A function from set $A$ to set $B$ assigns each element of $A$ exactly one element of $B$. Written: $f: A \to B$.Here:$A$ is the domain.$B$ is the codomain.$f(a)$ is the output assigned to $a$.Example: $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = x^2$.5.2 Domain, Codomain, and Range 📍For $f: A \to B$:Domain: $A$Codomain: $B$Range/Image: actual set of outputsExample: $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x)=x^2$. Range is $[0,\infty)$ because squares are never negative. Important: The codomain and range are not always the same.5.3 Functions as Relations 🔗A function is a special kind of relation. A relation $R \subseteq A \times B$ is a function if:For every $a \in A$, there exists exactly one $b \in B$ such that $(a,b) \in R$.5.4 Injective Functions 🏹A function is injective, or one-to-one, if different inputs give different outputs.Formal definition: $f \text{ is injective} \iff \forall x,y \in A, f(x)=f(y) \to x=y$.Example: $f(x)=2x+1$ is injective.5.5 Non-Injective Example ❌$f(x)=x^2$ is not injective because $f(2)=4$ and $f(-2)=4$, but $2 \ne -2$.5.6 Surjective Functions 🗺️A function is surjective, or onto, if every element of the codomain is hit by the function.Formal definition: $f: A \to B \text{ is surjective} \iff \forall b \in B, \exists a \in A \text{ such that } f(a)=b$.Example: $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x)=x^3$ is surjective. (Choose $x = \sqrt[3]{y}$).5.7 Non-Surjective Example 🚫$f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x)=x^2$ is not surjective because no real $x$ satisfies $x^2 = -1$.But if we define $f: \mathbb{R} \to [0,\infty), f(x)=x^2$, then $f$ is surjective. This shows surjectivity depends on the codomain.5.8 Bijective Functions 💎A function is bijective if it is both injective and surjective. A bijection pairs elements of $A$ and $B$ perfectly. If there is a bijection, $A$ and $B$ have the same size.Example: $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, f(x)=x+1$.5.9 Inverse Functions ⏪A function has an inverse if it is bijective.If $f: A \to B$ is bijective, then there exists $f^{-1}: B \to A$ such that $f^{-1}(f(a)) = a$ and $f(f^{-1}(b)) = b$.Example: $f(x)=2x+3$. The inverse is $f^{-1}(y)=\frac{y-3}{2}$.5.10 Composition of Functions 🔄If $f: A \to B$ and $g: B \to C$, their composition is $g \circ f: A \to C$ defined by $(g \circ f)(x)=g(f(x))$.Usually $g \circ f \ne f \circ g$. Order matters (highly important in quantum computing).5.11 Identity Function 🪞The identity function on $A$ is $id_A: A \to A$ defined by $id_A(x)=x$. It does nothing.In quantum computing, the identity operator represents “do nothing.”5.12 Image and Preimage 📸Let $f: A \to B$.Image of $S \subseteq A$: $f(S)=\{f(x): x \in S\}$Preimage of $T \subseteq B$: $f^{-1}(T)=\{x \in A: f(x) \in T\}$5.13 Functions in Cryptography 🔐Cryptography is full of functions.Symmetric encryption: $Enc: \mathcal{K} \times \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{C}$, $Dec: \mathcal{K} \times \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{M}$. Correctness requires $\forall k \in \mathcal{K}, \forall m \in \mathcal{M}, Dec(k, Enc(k,m)) = m$.Hash functions map arbitrary-length bitstrings to fixed-length bitstrings.5.14 Functions in Quantum Computing ⚛️Quantum gates are functions, but with extra structure.A quantum gate on $n$ qubits is a unitary operator: $U: \mathbb{C}^{2^n} \to \mathbb{C}^{2^n}$.Quantum operations are composed: $U_3 U_2 U_1|\psi\rangle$. Order matters!6. Proof Methods 📜A proof is a logical argument showing that a statement must be true. Mathematics is not about believing examples. It is about proving general claims.6.1 What Is a Proof? 🕵️‍♂️A proof starts from accepted facts and uses valid reasoning to reach a conclusion.Example claim: The sum of two even integers is even.Proof: Let $a$ and $b$ be even integers. Then there exist integers $m,n$ such that $a=2m$ and $b=2n$. Then $a+b=2m+2n=2(m+n)$. Since $m+n$ is an integer, $a+b$ is divisible by 2. Therefore $a+b$ is even.6.2 Direct Proof 🎯A direct proof starts with the hypothesis and logically reaches the conclusion.6.3 Proof by Contrapositive 🔄To prove $p \to q$, prove the equivalent statement $\neg q \to \neg p$.6.4 Proof by Contradiction 💥To prove a statement $p$:Assume $\neg p$.Derive a contradiction.Conclude $p$ must be true.6.5 Classic Contradiction Proof: $\sqrt{2}$ Is Irrational 📐Assume for contradiction that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational ($\frac{a}{b}$ in lowest terms).Square both sides: $2=\frac{a^2}{b^2}$, so $a^2=2b^2$.Thus $a^2$ is even, making $a$ even ($a=2k$).Substitute: $4k^2=2b^2 \implies 2k^2=b^2$.So $b^2$ is even, making $b$ even. Both have a common factor of 2, contradicting lowest terms!6.6 Proof by Cases 🗂️Sometimes a statement naturally splits into cases (e.g., proving a property for both even and odd integers separately).6.7 Proof of “If and Only If” ↔️To prove $p \leftrightarrow q$, prove both $p \to q$ and $q \to p$.6.8 Existence Proofs ✨To prove $\exists x, P(x)$, you only need to find one example (Constructive proof).6.9 Nonconstructive Existence Proofs 👻Sometimes we prove something exists without explicitly finding it, often by exhausting cases (e.g., proving irrational $a,b$ exist such that $a^b$ is rational).6.10 Uniqueness Proofs 🥇To prove uniqueness, show:ExistenceUniqueness (Assume two solutions $x, y$ exist, then prove $x=y$).6.11 Disproof by Counterexample 💣To disprove a universal statement $\forall x, P(x)$, find one $x$ for which $P(x)$ is false.6.12 Common Proof Mistakes ⚠️Proving by examples (Examples show possibility, not certainty).Assuming what you need to prove (Circular reasoning).Confusing converse with original.Negating incorrectly ($\neg(x > 5)$ is $x \le 5$, not $x < 5$).Ignoring domain (Check conditions!).7. Mathematical Induction 🪜Mathematical induction is a proof technique for statements indexed by natural numbers.7.1 Basic Idea of Induction 🧱Like dominoes lined up:The first domino falls.Whenever one domino falls, the next one falls.Then all dominoes fall.To prove a statement $P(n)$ for all $n \ge n_0$:Base case: Prove $P(n_0)$.Inductive step: Prove that for arbitrary $k \ge n_0$, $P(k) \to P(k+1)$.Conclusion: $P(n)$ is true for all $n \ge n_0$.7.2 Structure of an Induction Proof 🏗️Step 1: Base Case (Show $P(n_0)$ is true).Step 2: Inductive Hypothesis (Assume $P(k)$ is true).Step 3: Inductive Step (Prove $P(k+1)$).Step 4: Conclusion.7.3 Example 1: Sum of First $n$ Natural Numbers ➕Claim: $1+2+3+\cdots+n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$7.4 Example 2: Powers of 2 📈Claim: $2^n \ge n+1$ for all $n \ge 0$.7.5 Example 3: Divisibility ➗Claim: $3 \mid (4^n - 1)$ for all $n \ge 1$.7.6 Strong Induction 💪In strong induction, the inductive step assumes all previous cases ($P(n_0),P(n_0+1),\dots,P(k)$).7.7 Strong Induction Example 🧬Every integer $n \ge 2$ has a prime factorization.7.8 Induction and Recursive Algorithms 🔄Induction is the mathematical version of recursion. To prove a recursive algorithm correct, you usually use induction.7.9 Induction in Quantum Computing ⚛️Suppose you have $n$ qubits. The state space has dimension $2^n$. This can be proved by induction (each added qubit doubles the basis states).8. Practice Problems 🏋️‍♂️10.1 Propositional LogicProblem 1: Let $p = \text{“5 is prime”}$, $q = \text{“5 is even”}$. Find the truth value of $p \land q$.Solution: $p$ is true, $q$ is false. So $p \land q$ is false.Problem 2: Find the contrapositive of: If $n$ is divisible by 6, then $n$ is divisible by 3.Solution: If $n$ is not divisible by 3, then $n$ is not divisible by 6.Problem 3: Negate $p \lor q$.Solution: By De Morgan’s law: $\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$.10.2 Predicate LogicProblem 4: Write formally: Every integer has a square that is nonnegative.Solution: $\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}, n^2 \ge 0$Problem 5: Negate $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, \exists y \in \mathbb{R}: y > x$Solution: $\exists x \in \mathbb{R} \text{ such that } \forall y \in \mathbb{R}, y \le x$Problem 6: Explain the difference: $\forall x \exists y: y > x$ and $\exists y \forall x: y > x$Solution: The first says every number has some bigger number (True over $\mathbb{R}$). The second says there is one number bigger than every number (False over $\mathbb{R}$).10.3 Set TheoryProblem 7: Let $A=\{1,2,3\}$, $B=\{3,4,5\}$. Find $A \cup B$ and $A \cap B$.Solution: $A \cup B=\{1,2,3,4,5\}$, $A \cap B=\{3\}$.Problem 8: Find the power set of $A=\{a,b\}$.Solution: $\mathcal{P}(A)=\{\emptyset,\{a\},\{b\},\{a,b\}\}$.Problem 9: Prove $A \cap B \subseteq A$.Solution: Let $x \in A \cap B$. By definition, $x \in A$ and $x \in B$. Therefore $x \in A$. So $A \cap B \subseteq A$.10.4 RelationsProblem 10: Let $R$ be the relation on integers defined by $aRb \iff a-b \text{ is even}$. Show that $R$ is an equivalence relation.Solution:Reflexive: $a-a=0$ (even). So $aRa$.Symmetric: If $aRb$, $a-b$ is even. Then $b-a=-(a-b)$ is even. So $bRa$.Transitive: If $aRb$ and $bRc$, $(a-b)+(b-c)=a-c$ is even. So $aRc$.10.5 FunctionsProblem 11: Is $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}, f(x)=x^2$ injective?Solution: No. $f(2)=4$ and $f(-2)=4$, but $2 \ne -2$.Problem 12: Is $f:\mathbb{R}\to[0,\infty), f(x)=x^2$ surjective?Solution: Yes. For every $y \in [0,\infty)$, choose $x=\sqrt{y}$. Then $f(x)=x^2=y$.Problem 13: Find the inverse of $f(x)=3x-5$.Solution: Let $y=3x-5 \implies y+5=3x \implies x=\frac{y+5}{3}$. So $f^{-1}(y)=\frac{y+5}{3}$.10.6 ProofsProblem 14: Prove that the product of two odd integers is odd.Solution: Let $a=2m+1$ and $b=2n+1$. Then $ab=(2m+1)(2n+1)=4mn+2m+2n+1=2(2mn+m+n)+1$. This is odd.Problem 15: Disprove: Every prime number is odd.Solution: Counterexample: 2 is prime, but 2 is even.10.7 InductionProblem 16: Prove by induction: $1+3+5+\cdots+(2n-1)=n^2$ for all $n \ge 1$.Solution:Base Case: $n=1$, $1=1^2$. True.Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $1+3+\cdots+(2k-1)=k^2$.Inductive Step: Prove for $k+1$: $(1+3+\cdots+(2k-1))+(2(k+1)-1) = k^2+(2k+1) = (k+1)^2$. True by induction!11.
